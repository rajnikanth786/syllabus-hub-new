## üîπ 2.1 Introduction ‚Äì What Are Mass and Weight?

These two words are often mixed up, but in science **they mean very different things**.

| Concept                    | **Mass**                                     | **Weight**                                                        |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What it tells us**       | How much **matter** or ‚Äústuff‚Äù an object has | The **force of gravity** pulling on that matter                   |
| **Type of quantity**       | **Scalar** (has size only)                   | **Vector** (has size and direction ‚Äì towards the centre of Earth) |
| **Symbol**                 | ( m )                                        | ( W )                                                             |
| **Measured in**            | kilograms (kg)                               | newtons (N)                                                       |
| **Measuring instrument**   | **Balance** or **mass scale**                | **Spring balance / Newton meter**                                 |
| **Changes with location?** | ‚ùå No ‚Äì mass is constant everywhere           | ‚úÖ Yes ‚Äì depends on local gravity strength                         |
| **Formula**                | ‚Äî                                            | ( W = m √ó g )                                                     |

---

## üß† Key Idea

> **Mass is what you have. Weight is how strongly gravity pulls on it.**

Imagine carrying a backpack:

* On Earth, gravity is strong ‚Üí it **feels heavy**.
* On the Moon, gravity is weaker ‚Üí **same backpack** but **feels lighter**.
* In space, with almost no gravity ‚Üí backpack **floats** but **mass stays the same**!

## üîπ 2.2 Understanding Gravity

### What is gravity?

Gravity is a **non-contact force** that pulls all objects toward each other.
The **Earth‚Äôs gravity** pulls everything toward its centre.

**Gravitational field strength (g):**

* The **force of gravity per kilogram of mass**
* Measured in **newtons per kilogram (N/kg)**

| Location | g (N/kg)  | Comment          |
| -------- | --------- | ---------------- |
| Earth    | 9.8 (‚âà10) | Strong pull      |
| Moon     | 1.6       | Weaker pull      |
| Mars     | 3.7       | Medium pull      |
| Jupiter  | 24.5      | Very strong pull |

---

### Why we have weight

When gravity acts on your mass, you feel **weight** ‚Äî a downward **force**.

> üí° *The more mass you have, the greater your weight.*

Example:
If you double your mass, your weight doubles too (on the same planet).

---

## üîπ 2.3 The Weight Formula

[
\textbf{Weight (W) = Mass (m) √ó Gravitational field strength (g)}
]

| Symbol | Quantity                     | Unit           |
| ------ | ---------------------------- | -------------- |
| ( W )  | Weight                       | Newtons (N)    |
| ( m )  | Mass                         | Kilograms (kg) |
| ( g )  | Gravitational field strength | N/kg           |

### ‚öôÔ∏è Examples

**1.** A 5 kg object on Earth:
[
W = 5 √ó 9.8 = 49 N
]

**2.** The same object on the Moon (( g = 1.6 )):
[
W = 5 √ó 1.6 = 8 N
]

So the object **weighs less** but **mass remains 5 kg**.

## üîπ 2.4 Measuring Mass and Weight

### Measuring **Mass**

* Use a **digital balance** or **beam balance**.
* Compares unknown mass to known ones.
* Works the same **anywhere in the universe**.

### Measuring **Weight**

* Use a **spring balance (Newton meter)**.
* The spring stretches more when a heavier object is hung from it.
* Works only where **gravity acts**.
* Shows force in **newtons (N)**.

## üîπ 2.5 Comparing Mass and Weight ‚Äì Real-World Examples

| Object     | Mass (kg) | Weight on Earth (N) | Weight on Moon (N) |
| ---------- | --------- | ------------------- | ------------------ |
| Apple      | 0.1       | 1                   | 0.16               |
| School bag | 5         | 50                  | 8                  |
| Person     | 50        | 490                 | 80                 |

**Notice:** As gravity decreases, weight decreases proportionally.

## üîπ 2.6 Mass, Weight, and the Space Environment

* In **space**, astronauts are said to be **weightless** because they are in **free fall** around Earth.
* They **still have mass**, so they resist changes in motion (inertia).
* Even in orbit, gravity is still acting ‚Äî it just causes continuous falling around Earth.

## üîπ 2.8 Weight and Mass in Everyday Life

### ‚öñÔ∏è In lifts (elevators)

When a lift accelerates upward, you feel **heavier** (more normal force).
When it accelerates downward, you feel **lighter**.
Your **mass** never changes, only the **forces acting on you**.

### üöÄ On planets

Space agencies must calculate the **weight** of landers on other planets to design **safe landings** and **thrusters**.

### ‚öΩ In sports

* Weight affects **how fast** objects fall and **how much force** they hit with.
* Footballs feel lighter on the Moon because gravity is weaker!

| ‚ÄúGravity and weight are the same thing.‚Äù | ‚ùå Gravity is the **field**, weight is the **force** due to it. |

## üîπ 2.10 Practise Using the Formula

### ‚úèÔ∏è Worked Example 1

A stone has a mass of 3 kg. Calculate its weight on Earth.
[

]

### ‚úèÔ∏è Worked Example 2

An astronaut weighs 800 N on Earth. What will they weigh on the Moon (( g = 1.6 ))?

Step 1 ‚Äì Find mass:
[
m = W √∑ g = 800 √∑ 9.8 ‚âà 81.6 \text{ kg}
]

Step 2 ‚Äì Weight on Moon:
[
W = 81.6 √ó 1.6 ‚âà 130.6 N
]

‚û°Ô∏è *So they weigh about 131 N on the Moon.*

### ‚úèÔ∏è Worked Example 3

[
m = W √∑ g = 245 √∑ 9.8 = 25 \text{ kg}

## üîπ 2.11 Gravity and Weight Graph

If you plot **Weight (N)** on the y-axis and **Mass (kg)** on the x-axis:

* It‚Äôs a **straight line** through the origin.
* The **gradient = gravitational field strength (g)**.

  * On Earth, slope ‚âà 9.8 N/kg.
  * On Moon, slope ‚âà 1.6 N/kg.

**Meaning:**
Weight is **directly proportional** to mass.

---

## üîπ 2.12 The Role of Weight in Free Fall

When an object falls:

* Weight pulls it downward.
* Air resistance pushes upward.
* As it falls faster, air resistance increases until it balances weight ‚Üí **terminal velocity**.
* Then it falls at a **steady speed**.

This connects to both **forces** and **mass‚Äìweight** concepts.
