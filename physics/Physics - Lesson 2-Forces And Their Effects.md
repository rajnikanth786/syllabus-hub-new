## üîπ 1.1 What Is a Force?

A **force** is a **push, pull, twist or stretch** that one object exerts on another.
Whenever you **change how something moves or what shape it has**, a force is involved.

Forces can:

* Start or stop motion
* Speed things up or slow them down
* Change direction
* Change shape (stretch, squash, bend)

**Symbol:** ( F )‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ**Unit:** Newton (N)
**Instrument:** *Newton meter* or *spring balance*

---

### üåç Everyday examples

| Example                     | Type of Force | Effect        |
| --------------------------- | ------------- | ------------- |
| Pushing a door              | Contact       | Opens it      |
| Gravity pulling a ball down | Non-contact   | Ball falls    |
| Brakes on a bike            | Friction      | Slows motion  |
| Stretching a spring         | Tension       | Changes shape |

## üîπ 1.3 Measuring Forces

* **Spring balance / Newton meter** measures how strongly a spring is stretched by a force.
* Always **zero** the scale before measuring.
* Read at **eye level** to avoid parallax errors.

## üîπ 1.4 Resultant Force ‚Äì The ‚ÄúOverall‚Äù Force

When several forces act on an object, we combine them to find the **resultant force**.

* **Balanced forces:** all forces cancel ‚Üí **no change** in motion.
* **Unbalanced forces:** not equal ‚Üí object **accelerates, decelerates, or changes direction**.

**Example 1 ‚Äì Balanced:**
Book on a table ‚Üí weight (down) = reaction (up) ‚áí no motion.

**Example 2 ‚Äì Unbalanced:**
Car engine pushes forward with 400 N, friction is 300 N.
‚Üí Resultant = 400 ‚àí 300 = **100 N forward**
‚Üí Car **accelerates** forward.

## üîπ 1.5 Newton‚Äôs First Law of Motion

> ‚ÄúAn object will stay at rest or move at a constant speed in a straight line unless acted on by an unbalanced force.‚Äù

**Examples:**

* A hockey puck slides on ice (low friction) until friction and air resistance slow it.
* Seat belts keep passengers from moving forward when the car suddenly stops (inertia).

## üîπ 1.6 Mass and Weight

| Term           | Meaning                       | Unit           | Depends on       |
| -------------- | ----------------------------- | -------------- | ---------------- |
| **Mass (m)**   | Amount of matter in an object | kilograms (kg) | Object itself    |
| **Weight (W)** | Force of gravity on that mass | newtons (N)    | Gravity strength |

[
\textbf{W = m √ó g}
]
Where ( g ) = 9.8 N/kg on Earth (‚âà 10 N/kg for easy maths).

**Example:**
A 5 kg object ‚Üí ( W = 5 √ó 9.8 = 49 N ).
On the Moon, ( g ‚âà 1.6 N/kg ), so weight = ( 5 √ó 1.6 = 8 N ).

**Key idea:**
Mass stays the same everywhere; weight changes with gravity.

## üîπ 1.7 Gravity

* Gravity pulls all objects **toward the centre of Earth**.
* It gives objects **weight** and causes **free fall**.
* On Earth, all objects fall with the **same acceleration** (ignoring air resistance).

---

## üîπ 1.8 Friction and Air Resistance

### ‚öôÔ∏è Friction

A **contact force** that acts **opposite** to motion when two surfaces touch.

**Effects:**

* Wears surfaces down and produces heat.
* Can be **useful** (grip, stopping vehicles) or **unhelpful** (wastes energy).

**Reduce friction:**
Use **lubricants (oil)**, **smooth surfaces**, or **ball bearings**.

---

### üå¨Ô∏è Air Resistance (Drag)

A type of **friction with air particles** acting opposite to movement.

* Depends on **speed**, **shape**, and **surface area**.
* Greater speed ‚Üí greater air resistance.

**Streamlining**:
Designing shapes (cars, planes, fish) so air flows smoothly to **reduce drag**.

**Parachutes:**
Increase air resistance to slow descent.

## üîπ 1.9 Balanced and Unbalanced Forces in Action

| ----------------- | ----------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Book on desk      | Weight = Reaction       | Stationary                         |
| Skydiver at start | Weight > Air resistance | Accelerates down                   |
| Skydiver later    | Weight = Air resistance | Constant speed (terminal velocity) |
| Rocket launch     | Thrust > Weight + drag  | Accelerates upward                 |

## üîπ 1.10 Hooke‚Äôs Law ‚Äì Stretching Springs

When a **spring** or elastic band is stretched:

* The **extension** increases with the applied **force**.
* As long as the spring is not overstretched,
  [
  \textbf{F = k √ó e}
  ]

| Symbol | Meaning         | Unit        |
| ------ | --------------- | ----------- |
| F      | Force           | Newtons (N) |
| k      | Spring constant | N/m         |
| e      | Extension       | metres (m)  |

**Experiment outline:**

1. Hang spring on clamp stand.
2. Add 1 N weights step by step.
3. Measure new length each time.
4. Plot Force vs Extension graph.

   * Straight line = obeys Hooke‚Äôs Law.
   * Beyond ‚Äúlimit of proportionality‚Äù = permanent deformation.

**Applications:** suspension systems, trampolines, weighing scales.

## üîπ 1.11 Free-Body Diagrams

Show **all forces** acting on an object using arrows:

* **Direction** ‚Üí shows where the force acts.
* **Length** ‚Üí shows size (magnitude).
* **Label** each arrow (weight, friction, thrust, upthrust, etc.).

**Example:**
A car moving at constant speed ‚Üí forward thrust = backward drag ‚Üí balanced.

---

## üîπ 1.12 Upthrust and Floating

* **Upthrust** is the **upward force** on objects in fluids (liquids or gases).
* Caused by **pressure differences** in the fluid.
* If upthrust = weight ‚Üí object floats.
* If weight > upthrust ‚Üí object sinks.

**Example:**
A ship floats because its average density (metal + air spaces) is less than water‚Äôs density.

---

## üîπ 1.13 Magnetic and Electrostatic Forces

* **Magnetic forces:** between **magnets** or **magnetic materials** (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt).

  * **Like poles repel**, **opposite poles attract**.
* **Electrostatic forces:** between **charged objects**.

  * **Like charges repel**, **opposites attract**.
  * Example: balloon sticking to wall after rubbing.

Both are **non-contact** forces.
